# Common manuscript quality contract

Apply this contract to every design route. Use the selected design-specific reference for requirements that depend on the study design.

## Publication prose and authoring records

- **Publication-facing prose** contains the clinically coherent, concise scientific narrative: supported context, performed methods, results, interpretation, limitations, and the minimum inline source placeholder required to avoid inventing a claim.
- **Authoring records** contain route receipts, checklist maps and mechanics, source inventories, artifact paths and SHA-256 hashes, quantitative-claim provenance, detailed unresolved-source requirements, issue status, and manuscript-bundle bookkeeping.
- Keep those records in their named tables, registers, maps, indexes, or generation notes. Do not turn the manuscript into an account of route selection, checklist completion, file lookup, hashing, source collection, or writing instructions.
- Preserve traceability and blocking status across the separation. A concise manuscript does not delete evidence: each supported quantitative claim remains mapped in the provenance artifact, and each minimal inline placeholder maps to its full unresolved-source record.

## Title and abstract

- Identify the population, scientific objective, and study design accurately.
- When results are available, report the primary denominator and the main quantitative result with its uncertainty.
- Distinguish association, phenotype discovery, causal estimation, development, internal validation, and external validation.
- Use the structure required by the verified target-journal or article-type instructions; do not impose a fixed section count.
- Reconcile every abstract number and claim with the manuscript and frozen source artifacts.

## Introduction

- Lead with the clinical question, decision, evidence gap, or scientific uncertainty before describing algorithmic novelty.
- Match each objective to the frozen protocol and analysis status.
- Support background claims with verified evidence records, and distinguish established evidence from the study hypothesis.

## Methods

- Report governance, data-source versions, setting, eligibility, analysis grain, the complete named time contract, missing-data handling, software, reproducibility information, protocol deviations, and the selected design-specific analysis contract.
- Trace every operational definition to a frozen protocol, codebook, or reviewed analysis artifact.
- Describe only methods that were performed; do not add a conventional method that is absent from the frozen analysis.

## Results

- Organize results around the scientific questions rather than code execution order.
- Report applicable denominators, event counts, estimates, uncertainty, missingness, and diagnostic evidence.
- Keep primary, secondary, sensitivity, subgroup, exploratory, and validation results distinguishable.
- Preserve null, negative, adverse, and failed-diagnostic results; do not suppress them because they weaken the preferred narrative.
- Trace every quantitative statement to a frozen table, figure, or reviewed result artifact.

## Discussion, limitations, and conclusion

- Interpret the primary estimand, target, phenotype, or descriptive quantity before statistical significance.
- Separate direct evidence, plausible interpretation, and speculation.
- State each supported limitation with the likely direction or scope of its impact; when direction cannot be established, say so rather than guessing.
- Calibrate the conclusion to the design strength, validation status, and intended use.
- Follow verified target-journal limits when applicable; otherwise do not impose fixed paragraph counts or word limits.

## Tables and figures

- Give each display one scientific purpose and state its population, denominator, units, uncertainty, and abbreviations as applicable.
- Reconcile labels, sample sizes, event counts, estimates, and model names with the narrative and supplement.
- Do not require p values in Table 1 or other descriptive baseline tables by default, and do not use such p values to select adjustment variables.
- Prefer effect sizes, absolute measures, standardized differences, and intervals according to the scientific question and design.
- Place diagnostics and large supporting matrices in the supplement only when doing so does not hide evidence needed to interpret the primary result.

## Reporting-guideline records

- Record each selected guideline's `name`, authoritative URL, publication or version, retrieval date, applicability decision, any target-journal override, and checklist-to-section/page mapping.
- Base applicability on the frozen design and the guideline's stated scope; record a reason when a guideline is not applicable or a target journal changes the expected set.
- Link each applicable checklist item to the manuscript or supplement section and page after pagination is available; record unresolved mappings explicitly.
- Do not copy checklist text into this reusable skill. A checklist map supports reporting completeness but does not substitute for methodological review or independent manuscript review.

## Cross-artifact consistency gate

- Run a final consistency check across the manuscript, supplement, tables and figures with their legends, bibliography, reporting-checklist map, quantitative-claim provenance, and issue register.
- Resolve mismatches in labels, denominators, event counts, estimates, uncertainty, citations, artifact versions, and review status before describing the manuscript as frozen or submission-ready.
- Keep an unresolved mismatch in the issue register and block the frozen or submission-ready claim until it is closed with evidence.
