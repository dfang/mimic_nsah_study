# Observational manuscript route

Apply the common manuscript quality contract, then use either the descriptive route or the association route named in the frozen study manifest. Do not merge the routes because a model or comparison appears in the results. Draft only from the frozen protocol, codebook, reviewed analysis, and versioned result artifacts; leave an explicit source placeholder when required evidence is absent.

## Shared observational evidence

- State the scientific question, setting, source versions, sampling frame, eligibility rules, study period, analysis unit, and complete time contract from the frozen upstream artifacts.
- Report the flow from the sampling frame to each analyzed population, including applicable record, episode, and unique-patient denominators.
- Explain whether repeated records were retained, deterministically selected, aggregated, or otherwise handled, and preserve the reviewed analysis's dependence structure and inference unit.
- Report variable and outcome coverage, missingness, and measurement opportunity for the relevant population and time windows. Do not present absence of a recorded value as clinical absence unless the frozen definition supports that interpretation.
- Trace every quantity, display, and conclusion to a frozen result artifact. Report only methods and sensitivity analyses that were performed; do not invent conventional additions during manuscript writing.
- Preserve null, negative, imprecise, and failed-diagnostic findings and distinguish primary, secondary, subgroup, sensitivity, and exploratory analyses.

## Descriptive route

Use this route when the frozen objective is to quantify population characteristics, data coverage, burden, distribution, or trends without estimating an exposure-outcome association.

### Methods

- Define the sampling frame and how the analyzed sample covers or differs from it, including setting, calendar period, eligibility, exclusions, and observation windows.
- Name the analysis unit and identify the number of unique patients when the unit is an admission, stay, measurement, or other repeated record. Describe repeated-record inclusion, selection, aggregation, or within-patient summarization exactly as performed.
- Define each descriptive quantity and its denominator. For rates or time trends, state the time scale, population at risk or opportunity count, and any smoothing or uncertainty method that the frozen analysis used.
- Describe completeness by variable and relevant time window, including structural non-applicability and measurement opportunity when available.
- When populations or periods are compared with standardized rates, report the frozen standardization method, reference population, weights, and uncertainty. Do not add standardization that was not performed.

### Results and displays

- Begin with coverage of the sampling frame and analyzed population: records, unique patients, exclusions, observation time, and repeated-record structure as applicable.
- Report burden, distributions, frequencies, rates, or trends with explicit denominators, units, and uncertainty supported by the frozen results. Keep counts and percentages paired where useful.
- Present completeness alongside the quantities it affects, and identify denominator changes across variables, periods, or displays.
- Use cohort or observation flow, completeness summaries, distribution displays, and trend plots only when they answer the frozen descriptive question. Label crude and standardized quantities distinctly.
- Do not manufacture exposure groups, effect estimates, prediction performance, baseline significance tests, or p-value-driven narratives for a descriptive study.

### Discussion and claim boundary

- Interpret the magnitude, distribution, coverage, or temporal pattern that was directly described before offering possible explanations.
- Keep conclusions descriptive. A difference in burden or a temporal pattern does not establish an association, predictive relationship, intervention effect, or cause unless that separate question was frozen and analyzed under its appropriate route.
- Discuss selection, incomplete capture, changing measurement opportunity, repeated sampling, coding changes, and standardization limits only to the extent supported by the study artifacts; do not guess the direction of bias.

## Association route

Use this route when the frozen objective estimates the association between a prespecified exposure or grouping variable and an outcome. Regression alone does not make the study causal.

### Methods

- Define the exposure, comparator, outcome, eligible population, time zero, exposure-assessment window, follow-up or outcome window, and analysis time scale from the frozen protocol and codebook.
- State the effect measure or estimand, target population, contrast, and model specification actually used. Keep conditional and marginal quantities distinct when the reviewed analysis does so.
- Identify prespecified confounders and distinguish them from mediators, colliders, precision variables, and descriptive covariates. Give the frozen scientific or causal rationale for adjustment.
- Never select adjustment variables or confounders from Table 1 p values, univariable p values, statistical significance, or automated screening. Baseline significance testing is not confounding assessment.
- Report how repeated patients or episodes were selected or modeled and how the inference unit, variance estimation, or correlation structure matches that choice.
- Report missing-data handling, multiplicity decisions, model diagnostics, interaction or subgroup handling, and planned sensitivity analyses exactly as performed.

### Results and displays

- Report analyzed denominators, exposure-group sizes, outcome events or frequencies, follow-up, and missingness before estimates.
- Distinguish unadjusted from adjusted estimates and identify the adjustment set for each model. Pair effect estimates with intervals and report absolute risks, rates, or differences when the frozen artifacts support them.
- Report diagnostics and planned sensitivity analyses with enough evidence to assess model behavior; retain null, inconsistent, adverse, and imprecise findings.
- Give baseline-description tables a descriptive purpose. Do not use Table 1 p values to justify covariate inclusion, model selection, balance, or the preferred narrative.
- Keep primary estimates, secondary outcomes, multiplicity-controlled results, subgroups, interactions, and exploratory analyses visibly distinct across text, tables, and figures.

### Discussion and claim boundary

- Interpret the prespecified effect measure or estimand as an association and discuss clinical magnitude and uncertainty rather than treating statistical significance as the result.
- Use association language unless a separate causal identification strategy, assumptions, estimand, and analysis were frozen and independently reviewed. Adjustment alone does not establish exchangeability, eliminate residual confounding, or license causal wording.
- Relate limitations to selection, measurement, missingness, time ordering, model specification, multiplicity, and unmeasured or residual confounding without claiming a direction that the evidence cannot support.

## Completion checks

- The selected route matches the frozen manifest and reviewed analysis; descriptive and association claims are not blended.
- Sampling frame, analysis unit, time contract, coverage, repeated records, denominators, missingness, and performed methods agree across text and displays.
- A descriptive manuscript contains no invented exposure effect, prediction claim, or causal explanation.
- An association manuscript names the exposure, outcome, effect measure or estimand, adjustment rationale, repeated-record handling, diagnostics, and sensitivity analyses supported by the frozen artifacts.
- No covariate was chosen or defended using Table 1 or univariable p values, and no causal claim exceeds the frozen identification strategy and review status.
- Every quantitative statement and conclusion is traceable to frozen upstream evidence; unresolved evidence is a visible placeholder rather than an invented method or result.
